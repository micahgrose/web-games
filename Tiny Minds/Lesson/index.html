Tiny Minds

Four little AIs you can train in your own browser — built from scratch, no libraries, no magic. Together they walk you from "a network that colours a box" all the way to a working miniature GPT, one honest step at a time.

Why bother with tiny ones?

Modern AI feels like a black box. The trick to understanding it isn't a bigger box — it's a smaller one. Each model here is small enough that nothing is hidden: you can watch every weight learn, see exactly what it's trained on, and catch it making mistakes. The ideas inside these toys are the same ideas inside the giant models — just shrunk until you can see them with your own eyes.

First, the basics

You don't need any background. Here's the whole vocabulary in plain language — open any box for a little more.

What is a "neural network"?

Imagine a giant mixing board with millions of little knobs. You feed something in at one end (a position, a word) and a result comes out the other end (a colour, the next word). The knobs decide how the input gets turned into the output. A neural network is exactly that: a big adjustable function made of simple math units ("neurons"), wired together, with the knobs being numbers called weights.

On its own it does nothing useful — the knobs start random, so the output is nonsense. The magic is in tuning them.

What is "training" (and a "training step")?

Training is how the knobs get tuned. You show the network an example, compare what it produced to the right answer, and measure how wrong it was — that number is the loss. Then you nudge every knob a tiny bit in the direction that would have made the loss smaller. That single show-example → measure → nudge cycle is one training step. Repeat it thousands of times and the network slowly gets good. That's all training is: a patient hill-climb down the error.

What is a "token", and what does "generating text" mean?

A computer can't read words directly, so we chop text into pieces called tokens — sometimes single letters, sometimes whole words. The model learns to predict the next token given the ones so far. To "generate", it predicts a token, adds it to the end, then predicts again, and again — building a reply one piece at a time. (Yes, that's how the big chatbots write too.)

Three words you'll see on every screen: loss, memorized, generalized

Loss — how wrong the model is right now (lower is better).

Memorized — how well it does on the exact examples it studied. Easy to make high… by cheating (just memorizing).

Generalized — how well it does on examples it has never seen. This is the honest score — it shows real understanding, not memorization. When "memorized" races ahead while "generalized" lags, the model is overfitting: cramming instead of learning.

How does the "nudging" actually work? (gradient descent)

Picture standing on a foggy hill, trying to reach the bottom. You can't see far, but you can feel which way the ground slopes under your feet — so you take a small step downhill, then feel again, and repeat. That's gradient descent, and it's the entire engine of learning. The "hill" is the loss (how wrong the model is); the "slope" is called the gradient — the math tells the model, for every single knob, which way to turn it to make the loss a little smaller. The size of each step is the learning rate: too big and you leap past the bottom; too small and you crawl forever.

That's it. No understanding, no intuition — just millions of tiny "which way is downhill?" steps. Do enough of them and a pile of random knobs turns into something that can write.

What is "attention"? (the idea behind modern AI)

When you read "the dog chased its tail because it was bored", you instantly know "it" means the dog, not the tail. You did that by paying attention to the right earlier word. That's exactly what attention does in an AI: for each new word it writes, it decides which earlier words matter and leans on those.

Older models read through a small fixed window and forgot the rest. Attention lets a model reach back to any earlier word that's relevant, however far back. That one trick — "look at what matters" — is what made today's chatbots possible. You meet a simple version in Chapter 3 and the real thing in Chapter 4.

What's a "transformer", and why is it called "GPT"?

A transformer is the modern design that stacks attention together in layers. Almost every big AI today is one. GPT stands for Generative (it makes new text) Pre-trained (it's trained on a big pile of data first, then used) Transformer (that design). When you press Train in these toys, you're doing the "pre-trained" part yourself; when you chat, you're using the finished model.

Glossary

Open the glossary

Every term below has its own link — and definitions cross-link to each other, so you can follow a thread. Words throughout the lesson point back here too.

weight — one tunable knob inside the network. Same thing as a parameter.

neuron — a tiny math unit that adds up its inputs and passes the result on.

loss — a number for how wrong the model is; training pushes it down.

step — one round of: show example, measure loss, nudge the knobs.

token — a chunk of text (a letter or a word) the model reads and writes.

overfitting — memorizing the training examples instead of learning the pattern, so generalization suffers.

confidence — how sure the model is of its own answer (high + wrong = a hallucination).

attention — the model deciding which earlier words matter for the next one.

head — one "spotlight" of attention, tracking one kind of relationship (Chapter 4).

layer — one stacked stage of processing; more layers = deeper understanding (Chapter 4).

— the training words —

parameter — another word for a weight; the millions of numbers a model learns.

gradient — the "downhill" direction that would reduce the loss; training follows it.

gradient descent — the whole learning method: keep stepping downhill on the loss, following the gradient.

learning rate — how big each nudge is. Too big overshoots, too small crawls.

batch — a handful of examples shown together before the knobs are nudged once.

epoch — one full pass over all of the training data.

capacity / ceiling — the most a given network can possibly learn; its size sets the limit. Pushing past it just causes overfitting.

— the language words —

vocabulary — the full set of tokens a model knows; unknown words become a special "unknown" token.

embedding — turning a token into a list of numbers the network can actually do math on.

context window / memory — how much recent text the model can look at while writing the next word.

softmax — turns raw scores into probabilities that add up to 100%.

temperature / creativity — how randomly it picks the next token; low = safe and repetitive, high = wild.

masking — hiding parts of the data from the loss, so the model only learns to write the right parts.

phase — the opening, middle, or ending of a chat; the bots learn each one separately.

— the big-picture words —

transformer — the modern architecture built on attention; the "T" in GPT.

GPT — Generative Pre-trained Transformer (Chapter 4).

pre-training — the long training done before a model is used. Pressing "Train" here is your pre-training.

inference — using a trained model to produce output (chatting), rather than training it.

generalization — doing well on examples never seen in training; the real test of learning.

hallucination — a confident, fluent answer that happens to be wrong.
